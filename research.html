Investment Tax Credits and the Response of Firms
November 2018 - JOB MARKET PAPER

This paper estimates separately direct and spillover effects of investment tax credits on input choices of firms by exploiting a shift in the tax credit rate of small compared to large manufacturing firms in Germany in 1999. I find that tax credits lead to a sizable increase in both investment and the number of employees of firms. While there is little evidence that the average firm adjusts its skill mix or occupation structure, firms in industries with a larger share of information and communication technology (ICT) are more likely to shift towards high-skilled labor and occupations. The employment effect materializes through the hiring of additional employees rather than a decrease in separations and the unemployed constitute about half of these hires. Local spillovers between firms lead to an additional positive employment effect and are therefore important when considering the cost effectiveness of tax credits. The effect tends to be stronger between firms that are in the same industry and is close to zero for the service sector.

The Recognition of Foreign Qualifications and Immigrant Labor Market Outcomes
with Herbert Brücker, Albrecht Glitz, Agnese Romiti
July 2018

In this paper, we analyze how the formal recognition of immigrants’ foreign occupational credentials affects their subsequent labor market outcomes. Our empirical analysis is based on a novel German data set that links respondents’ survey information to their administrative records, allowing us to observe immigrants at monthly intervals before, during and after their application for occupational recognition. To address the inherent issue of self-selection into the application process, we focus on a sample of immigrants who eventually all receive full recognition, and exploit variation in the timing of the recognition process. As a robustness check, we further employ a pooled synthetic control group approach, matching workers who obtain full recognition to control groups of workers with similar pre-recognition labor market trajectories. Our empirical findings show substantial employment and wage gains from occupational recognition, both in the short and in the long run. After three years, for immigrants who obtained full recognition the share of days in employment per month is around 26.3 percentage points higher and they earn 40.1 percent higher hourly wages than immigrants without recognition. We show that the increase in employment is driven almost exclusively by a higher propensity to work in regulated occupations. Relating our findings to the relative earnings position of immigrants in Germany, we finally show that occupational recognition leads to a substantially faster convergence of immigrants’ earnings to those of their native counterparts.

Capital Adjustment Costs and the Elasticity of Substitution: Evidence from Tax Credits

In this paper, I use a tax policy in Germany to simultaneously estimate the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor, and capital adjustment costs. The program provided investment tax credits to firms to reduce investment costs. I exploit the reduction in the tax credit rate at a firm size cutoff of 250 employees and analyze the bunching behavior of firms. I set up a dynamic model of firm investment and employment with capital adjustment costs and size-dependent capital costs and estimate policy parameters and firm behavior around the cutoff using simulated method of moments.

Immigration and the Adjustment Behavior of Firms

This paper examines the effect of immigration in a unified theoretical framework of the labor and product market. Immigrants increase the labor supply in the host country but also raise the demand for consumption goods. These changes influence both firms’ input and output decisions. I focus on the trading behavior between local labor markets in a model with heterogeneous firms similar to Melitz (2003). Firms profit from an inflow of immigrants in their close proximity on the product market, since they can reach more individuals close by without incurring trade costs. To keep labor employed in all regions, wages build a counteracting force and increase in labor markets with an inflow of immigrants.
